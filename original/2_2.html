The vehicle purchase process is normally considered to consist of three phases. This phases also constitute the top level abstractions of the relevant business processes and sub-processes:

  • the information phase where a potential buyer gathers information about a vehicle or vehicles; this generally starts outside a dealership, as the customer comes into contact with a car family on television, in newspapers or magazines, or perhaps sees it on the road. It continues as interest in the car deepens, and more information is gathered until detailed information (perhaps a trial, or potential trade-in values) needs to be obtained, at which point a dealer becomes the focus and main source of additional information;
  • the negotiation phase where a specific configuration needs to be agreed and priced, and perhaps delivery dates need to be checked. This is the point at which the trade-in value is firmed and the final price defined;
  • the closure and transaction phase where the terms of the deal are not only firmed totally, but recorded and an order is placed for a vehicle

Classically these business sub-processes have been carried out at a car dealership almost in their totality, since the dealer has traditionally been the best and often only source of information about a car (brochures). It is only in the past three years that detailed information about cars has started appearing on bulletin boards (Compuserve, AOL etc.) or the World Wide Web.

The vehicle purchase process is not transactional in nature. Like any other process that is based on communication and negotiation between two persons, it moves at human speeds, and suffers from the vagaries of human nature in terms of the lack of sequencing structure; what is important to one customer may be completely unimportant to another.

This is one reason why existing on-line approaches have turned out to be rather static and inflexible; their greatest problem is that they do not deploy sufficient technology to properly support the way in which the component objects of the vehicle purchase process interact in different combinations at different times.
