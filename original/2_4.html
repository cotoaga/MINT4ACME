Since the mid-1990s, trends such as continuous process improvement, and business-process reengineering have underscored the need for flexible, open business applications that can be deployed into a continuously evolving matrix of businesses, partnerships, customers and technology. To meet these demands, modern IT applications must be able to run on a variety of different hardware and software platforms while being instantly deployable and maintainable across an increasingly decentralized IT infrastructure that covers central data warehouses and mainframes, departmental servers, desktop and mobile computers, as well as special-purpose or next-generation equipment.

For the MINT-Application we see the following further requirements:

  • low overall cost of implementation and ownership, as well as use commodity technologies and skill sets;
  • use open standards so that they are flexible enough to co-operate with existing applications from different vendors today and in the future;
  • leverage existing infrastructure investments in desktop computers, servers, mainframes, databases, applications, and networks;
  • scale seamlessly from the LAN to the WAN, enabling the exposure of business processes through the corporate firewall to connect business applications directly to customers, suppliers, and partners;
  • be deployed and managed centrally from a server rather than requiring costly updates to static, individual LANs, desktops or laptops;
  • support component reusability, increasing ACME's ability to assemble new applications rapidly from existing services.

These requirements are best satisfied by the use of Internet technologies and standards, which are currently leading one of the most remarkable software revolutions for IT departments since the introduction of the personal computer. Internet standards are becoming an important development environment for a host of new enterprise applications. From its genesis as an open, information-sharing medium, the Internet is evolving into a full application development platform, fostering a transformation in application design and implementation that is as significant as the shift from host-based application development to the desktop-centric paradigm offered by the client-server model. This new class of platform-independent applications, called network-centric applications, combines the advantages of central control and administration with the power and flexibility of distributed applications.

We will develop MINT as a network-centric, browser-based application consisting of a series of ActiveX controls or Java Applets and different Plug-ins. These Controls will be used to represent business objects, processes and sub-processes, database tables and multimedia information streams.

The controls will be held together by a glue that will initially be either the Microsoft Internet Explorer or Netscape Communicator but may later be just a part of the operating system once Microsoft releases its HTML-enabled Windows desktop.

The controls will display multimedia objects of various types and interact with the underlying systems. The maintenance and the process logic will be provided by an Application and a Replication Server. These server systems provide the encapsulated business logic.
